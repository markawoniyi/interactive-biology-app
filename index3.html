Day 1: What Makes Something Alive?

Unit 1, Week 1 • The Seven Characteristics of Life

Learn what makes something truly "alive" in the biological sense

🎯 Today's Objectives

  • List and explain the 7 characteristics of life
  • Give examples of each characteristic in different organisms
  • Distinguish between living and non-living things

How do we know if something is alive? Scientists have identified seven key characteristics that all living things share. These characteristics help us distinguish between living organisms and non-living objects.

Every living thing, from the tiniest bacteria to the largest whale, exhibits these seven characteristics: organization, metabolism, homeostasis, growth, reproduction, response to environment, and adaptation through evolution.

🔑 The Seven Characteristics

1. Organization

Living things have complex, ordered structures made of cells.

2. Metabolism

Chemical reactions that provide energy and build materials.

3. Homeostasis

Maintaining stable internal conditions despite external changes.

4. Growth

Increase in size and complexity over time.

5. Reproduction

Ability to produce offspring and pass on genetic information.

6. Response to Environment

React to changes in surroundings (stimuli).

7. Adaptation through Evolution

Species change over time to better survive in their environment.

Day 2: The Scientific Method

Unit 1, Week 1 • How Scientists Study Life

Master the systematic approach scientists use to understand nature

🎯 Today's Objectives

  • Describe the steps of the scientific method
  • Design a simple biological experiment
  • Distinguish between observations and inferences
  • Identify variables in an experiment

The scientific method is a systematic way of learning about the natural world. It helps scientists ask questions, make predictions, and test their ideas through careful observation and experimentation.

By following the scientific method, biologists can discover how living things work, from the smallest molecules to entire ecosystems.

🔬 Scientific Method Steps

1
Observation

Notice something interesting in nature using your senses

2
Question

Ask a testable question about what you observed

3
Hypothesis

Make an educated guess about the answer (if...then...because)

4
Experiment

Test your hypothesis with controlled conditions

5
Analyze & Conclude

Examine results and determine if hypothesis was supported

Day 4: Enzymes & Chemical Reactions

Unit 1, Week 3 • Biological Catalysts

Discover how enzymes make the chemistry of life possible

🎯 Today's Objectives

  • Explain what enzymes are and how they work
  • Describe the lock-and-key model of enzyme action
  • Identify factors that affect enzyme activity
  • Understand the importance of enzymes in living organisms

Enzymes are special proteins that speed up chemical reactions in living organisms. Without enzymes, the chemical reactions necessary for life would happen too slowly to sustain life.

Every cell contains thousands of different enzymes, each designed to catalyze specific reactions. They make life's chemistry possible by lowering the energy needed to start reactions.

🔧 How Enzymes Work

Lock-and-Key Model
  • • Enzyme = lock, Substrate = key
  • • Substrate fits into enzyme's active site
  • • Perfect fit allows reaction to occur
  • • Products are released, enzyme reused
Catalytic Action
  • • Lowers activation energy needed
  • • Speeds up reaction rate
  • • Not consumed in the reaction
  • • Can be used over and over

🌡️ Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

Temperature

Higher temperature increases molecular motion and reaction rate

Too hot: enzyme denatures (loses shape and function)

pH Level

Each enzyme has an optimal pH range

Wrong pH: changes enzyme shape, reduces activity

Substrate Concentration

More substrate = faster reaction (up to a point)

Saturation point: all enzyme active sites occupied

🧬 Important Biological Enzymes

Digestive Enzymes
  • • Amylase: breaks down starch
  • • Pepsin: breaks down proteins
  • • Lipase: breaks down fats
Cellular Enzymes
  • • Catalase: breaks down hydrogen peroxide
  • • DNA polymerase: copies DNA
  • • ATP synthase: makes cellular energy

Explore the chemical foundation that makes life possible

  • Identify the structure of atoms and their components
  • Explain the difference between elements and compounds
  • Describe the three main types of chemical bonds

All living things are made of matter, and matter is composed of atoms. Understanding basic chemistry helps us understand how biological molecules form and function in living organisms.

The chemistry of life involves just a few key elements that combine in countless ways to create the complex molecules that make life possible.

⚛️ Atomic Structure

Protons
  • • Positive charge (+)
  • • Located in nucleus
  • • Determines element identity
  • • Mass = 1 atomic mass unit
Neutrons
  • • No charge (neutral)

Electrons
  • • Negative charge (-)
  • • Orbit around nucleus
  • • Involved in chemical bonds
  • • Nearly no mass

Day 3: Levels of Organization

Unit 1, Week 1 • Biological Hierarchy

From atoms to ecosystems: understanding the complexity of life

🎯 Today's Objectives

  • Describe the different levels of biological organization
  • Explain how each level builds upon the previous one
  • Give examples of each level in living organisms

Life is organized in a hierarchical structure, from the simplest atomic particles to the complexity of ecosystems. Each level of organization builds upon the previous one, allowing scientists to study life at different scales.

The major levels of biological organization are: atom, molecule, cell, tissue, organ, organ system, organism, population, community, ecosystem, and biosphere.

🏗️ Biological Hierarchy

Atoms & Molecules

Atoms combine to form molecules, the chemical building blocks of life (e.g., water, glucose).

Cells

The basic unit of life. All living things are made of cells.

Tissues

Groups of similar cells working together (e.g., muscle tissue).

Organs

Structures made of different tissues performing specific functions (e.g., heart, leaf).

Organ Systems

Groups of organs working together (e.g., digestive system).

Organism

An individual living thing (e.g., human, tree, bacterium).

Population

All members of one species in an area.

Community & Ecosystem

Community: All populations in an area. Ecosystem: All living things plus the non-living environment.

Biosphere

The entire planet where life exists.

Day 1: Microscopes & Magnification

Unit 1, Week 2 • Scientific Tools

Tools for seeing the microscopic world

🎯 Today's Objectives

  • Identify common types of microscopes
  • Explain how microscopes work and why they're important
  • Calculate total magnification

Microscopes allow scientists to see things too small for the naked eye—like cells and bacteria. Different microscopes help us study life at different levels of detail.

  • Light microscope: Uses light and lenses to enlarge images. Good for cells and tissues.
  • Stereo microscope: Makes 3D images, great for dissection.
  • Electron microscope: Uses electrons for super high detail, like viruses or cell parts.

Total magnification = eyepiece × objective lens.

Day 2: Water & Its Properties

Unit 1, Week 3 • The Molecule of Life

Exploring the unique characteristics of water

🎯 Today's Objectives

  • Describe the structure of water molecules
  • Explain why water is essential for life
  • Identify key properties of water (cohesion, adhesion, etc.)
  • Relate water’s properties to biological processes

Water is the most important molecule for life. The unique way its atoms bond gives water special properties that support all living things.

  • Structure: Two hydrogen atoms + one oxygen atom (H2O), held together by covalent bonds.
  • Polarity: Water has a slight positive end and a slight negative end, making it great at dissolving substances.

🌊 Key Properties of Water

  • Cohesion: Water molecules stick to each other (helps plants move water up stems).
  • Adhesion: Water sticks to other surfaces (important for transport in living things).
  • High specific heat: Water absorbs lots of energy before changing temperature—helps organisms maintain temperature.
  • Universal solvent: Water dissolves many substances, making it vital for biochemical reactions.

Water’s properties make it essential for processes like temperature regulation, transporting nutrients, and chemical reactions inside cells.

Day 3: Organic Molecules

Unit 1, Week 3 • Macromolecules

Carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids

🎯 Today's Objectives

  • Identify the four major types of organic molecules
  • Describe the functions of each molecule in living things
  • Recognize examples of carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids

Organic molecules are the building blocks of life. All living things are made up of four main groups:

  • Carbohydrates: Sugars and starches. Provide quick energy and structure (e.g., glucose, cellulose).
  • Lipids: Fats and oils. Store energy, make cell membranes, insulate (e.g., triglycerides, phospholipids).
  • Proteins: Made of amino acids. Build body structures, speed up reactions (enzymes), transport materials (e.g., hemoglobin, keratin).
  • Nucleic acids: DNA & RNA. Store and carry genetic information.

🔬 Why They Matter

Each group plays a special role in keeping organisms alive and functioning. They control growth, energy use, reproduction, and more!

Unit 2, Week 1, Day 1: Cell Theory

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • State the three principles of cell theory
  • • Explain the historical development of cell theory
  • • Identify key scientists who contributed to cell theory
  • • Describe why cell theory is fundamental to biology

🔬 The Foundation of Biology

Cell theory is one of the most important theories in biology. It describes the properties and characteristics that all living things share at the cellular level.

The Three Principles of Cell Theory:

  1. All living things are made of one or more cells - From the smallest bacteria to the largest whale, every living organism is composed of cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of life - Cells are the smallest structures that can perform all life functions.
  3. All cells come from existing cells - New cells are created when existing cells divide, not from non-living matter.

Historical Development:

1665 - Robert Hooke

First to observe and name "cells" while looking at cork under a microscope.

1674 - Anton van Leeuwenhoek

First to observe living cells, including bacteria and protists.

1838-1839 - Schleiden & Schwann

Proposed that all plants and animals are made of cells.

1855 - Rudolf Virchow

Stated that all cells come from pre-existing cells.

🎯 Learning Check

Can you explain why cell theory is considered a unifying principle in biology?

Think about how it connects all living things and provides a foundation for understanding life processes.

Unit 2, Week 1, Day 2: Prokaryotic Cells

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Define prokaryotic cells and their key characteristics
  • • Compare bacteria and archaea as examples of prokaryotes
  • • Identify structural features unique to prokaryotic cells
  • • Explain the ecological importance of prokaryotes

🦠 The Simplest Cells

Prokaryotic cells are the oldest and simplest form of life on Earth. The word "prokaryote" means "before nucleus" because these cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus.

Key Characteristics:

  • • No nucleus: DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm in a region called the nucleoid
  • • No membrane-bound organelles: Structures like mitochondria and chloroplasts are absent
  • • Small size: Typically 1-5 micrometers in diameter
  • • Cell wall: Most have a rigid cell wall made of peptidoglycan
  • • Ribosomes: Smaller (70S) ribosomes for protein synthesis

Examples of Prokaryotes:

🦠 Bacteria
  • • E. coli (found in intestines)
  • • Streptococcus (causes strep throat)
  • • Lactobacillus (makes yogurt)
  • • Nitrogen-fixing bacteria
🌋 Archaea
  • • Methanogens (produce methane)
  • • Halophiles (love salt)
  • • Thermophiles (love heat)
  • • Found in extreme environments

🔍 Important Functions

♻️
Decomposition

Break down dead organisms

🌱
Nitrogen Fixation

Convert atmospheric nitrogen

🍞
Food Production

Make yogurt, cheese, bread

Unit 2, Week 1, Day 3: Eukaryotic Cells

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Define eukaryotic cells and their key characteristics
  • • Compare prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell structures
  • • Identify membrane-bound organelles in eukaryotic cells
  • • Distinguish between plant and animal cell features

🧬 Complex Cellular Organization

Eukaryotic cells are more complex than prokaryotic cells. The word "eukaryote" means "true nucleus" because these cells have a membrane-bound nucleus that contains their DNA.

Key Characteristics:

  • • Membrane-bound nucleus: DNA is enclosed within a nuclear envelope
  • • Membrane-bound organelles: Specialized structures for different functions
  • • Larger size: Typically 10-100 micrometers in diameter
  • • Complex internal structure: Compartmentalized for efficiency
  • • Larger ribosomes: 80S ribosomes for protein synthesis
🌱 Plant Cells
  • • Cell wall (cellulose)
  • • Chloroplasts for photosynthesis
  • • Large central vacuole
  • • Examples: trees, flowers, algae
🐾 Animal Cells
  • • No cell wall
  • • No chloroplasts
  • • Small vacuoles
  • • Examples: humans, fish, insects

🧱 The Cell's Gatekeeper

The cell membrane is like a security gate that controls what enters and exits the cell. Its unique structure allows it to be both protective and selective.

Phospholipid Bilayer Structure:

🔵 Phospholipids
  • • Hydrophilic (water-loving) head
  • • Hydrophobic (water-fearing) tails
  • • Form double layer automatically
  • • Fluid and flexible structure
🟡 Membrane Proteins
  • • Transport proteins (channels/carriers)
  • • Recognition proteins (cell identity)
  • • Receptor proteins (receive signals)
  • • Enzyme proteins (catalyze reactions)

Key Properties:

🚪
Selective Permeability

Only certain substances can pass through

🌊
Fluid Mosaic

Flexible with proteins floating like icebergs

🛡️
Protection

Maintains cell shape and internal environment

🚛 Moving Materials In and Out

Cells need to move materials across their membranes to survive. Different transport methods are used depending on the size of molecules and energy requirements.

🔄 Passive Transport (No Energy)

Diffusion

Movement from high to low concentration

Osmosis

Water movement across membrane

Facilitated Diffusion

Uses protein channels/carriers

⚡ Active Transport (Uses Energy)

Protein Pumps

Move against concentration gradient

Endocytosis

Cell engulfs large particles

Exocytosis

Cell expels waste or products

Unit 2, Week 2, Day 3: Cellular Respiration

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Write the overall equation for cellular respiration
  • • Explain the three stages of cellular respiration
  • • Describe where cellular respiration occurs in the cell
  • • Understand the role of ATP as cellular energy currency

⚡ Cellular Energy Production

Cellular respiration is how cells break down glucose to make ATP energy. This process occurs in all living organisms and is essential for life.

The Overall Equation:

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP

Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water + Energy

Three Stages of Cellular Respiration:

1. Glycolysis (Cytoplasm)
  • • Glucose broken into 2 pyruvate molecules
  • • Produces 2 ATP and 2 NADH
  • • Does not require oxygen (anaerobic)
2. Krebs Cycle (Mitochondrial Matrix)
  • • Pyruvate fully broken down
  • • Produces 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH₂
  • • Requires oxygen (aerobic)
3. Electron Transport Chain (Inner Membrane)
  • • NADH and FADH₂ used to make ATP
  • • Produces about 32 ATP
  • • Oxygen is final electron acceptor
Total Energy Yield:

One glucose molecule can produce approximately 36-38 ATP molecules through cellular respiration!

Unit 2, Week 2, Day 4: Photosynthesis

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Write the overall equation for photosynthesis
  • • Explain the two stages of photosynthesis (light and dark reactions)
  • • Describe the role of chloroplasts and chlorophyll
  • • Compare photosynthesis and cellular respiration

🌱 Capturing Solar Energy

Photosynthesis is how plants and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy (glucose). This process provides energy for almost all life on Earth!

The Overall Equation:

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Light Energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Carbon Dioxide + Water + Light → Glucose + Oxygen

Two Stages of Photosynthesis:

Light Reactions (Thylakoids)
  • • Chlorophyll captures light energy
  • • Water molecules are split
  • • Oxygen is released as waste
  • • ATP and NADPH are produced
Calvin Cycle (Stroma)
  • • CO₂ is "fixed" into organic molecules
  • • Uses ATP and NADPH from light reactions
  • • Produces glucose (sugar)
  • • Does not directly require light

Photosynthesis vs. Cellular Respiration:

🌱 Photosynthesis
  • • Builds glucose (anabolic)
  • • Uses CO₂, produces O₂
  • • Stores energy
  • • Only in plants/some bacteria
⚡ Cellular Respiration
  • • Breaks down glucose (catabolic)
  • • Uses O₂, produces CO₂
  • • Releases energy
  • • In all living organisms

Unit 2, Week 3, Day 1: Cell Cycle

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Describe the main phases of the cell cycle
  • • Explain what happens during interphase (G1, S, G2)
  • • Identify the purpose of cell cycle checkpoints
  • • Understand how the cell cycle is regulated

⏰ The Life of a Cell

The cell cycle is the series of events that cells go through as they grow and divide. Most of a cell's life is spent growing and preparing for division.

Main Phases of the Cell Cycle:

Interphase (90% of cell cycle)
G₁ Phase

Cell grows and carries out normal functions

S Phase

DNA replication occurs

G₂ Phase

Cell prepares for division

M Phase (Mitotic Phase)
  • • Mitosis: Nuclear division (chromosomes separate)
  • • Cytokinesis: Cytoplasm divides, forming 2 cells
  • • Results in two identical daughter cells

Cell Cycle Checkpoints:

  • • G₁/S Checkpoint: Is the cell large enough and ready for DNA replication?
  • • G₂/M Checkpoint: Is DNA replicated correctly?
  • • Spindle Checkpoint: Are all chromosomes attached to spindle fibers?

Unit 2, Week 3, Day 2: Mitosis

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Identify the four phases of mitosis (PMAT)
  • • Describe what happens to chromosomes during each phase
  • • Explain the role of the spindle apparatus
  • • Understand the purpose and result of mitotic division

🧬 Nuclear Division

Mitosis is the process by which a cell's nucleus divides to create two identical nuclei. This ensures each new cell gets exactly the same genetic information.

The Four Phases of Mitosis:

1. Prophase
  • • Chromosomes condense and become visible
  • • Nuclear envelope breaks down
  • • Spindle fibers begin to form
  • • Centrioles move to opposite poles
2. Metaphase
  • • Chromosomes line up at cell center
  • • Forms the metaphase plate
  • • Each chromosome attached to spindle
  • • Cell checks all attachments
3. Anaphase
  • • Sister chromatids separate
  • • Chromosomes move to opposite poles
  • • Spindle fibers shorten
  • • Cell elongates
4. Telophase
  • • Nuclear envelopes reform
  • • Chromosomes uncoil
  • • Spindle fibers disappear
  • • Cytokinesis begins
🎯 Purpose of Mitosis:
  • • Growth: Adding new cells to increase organism size
  • • Repair: Replacing damaged or dead cells
  • • Asexual reproduction: Creating identical offspring

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Explain the two divisions of meiosis (I and II)

🎲 Sexual Reproduction Division

Meiosis is a special type of cell division that produces gametes (sex cells) with half the chromosome number. This creates genetic diversity essential for sexual reproduction.

Key Differences: Mitosis vs. Meiosis

🔄 Mitosis
  • • One division
  • • 2 identical diploid cells
  • • Same chromosome number
  • • Growth and repair
  • • No genetic recombination
🎲 Meiosis
  • • Two divisions
  • • 4 unique haploid cells
  • • Half chromosome number
  • • Sexual reproduction
  • • Crossing over creates diversity

Sources of Genetic Diversity:

Crossing Over

Homologous chromosomes exchange genetic material during prophase I

Independent Assortment

Chromosomes randomly separate into different gametes

Fertilization

Random combination of gametes from two parents

🧬 The Molecule of Life

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) contains the genetic instructions for all living things. It's organized into structures called chromosomes within the cell nucleus.

Double Helix
  • • Two twisted strands
  • • Sugar-phosphate backbone
  • • Antiparallel orientation
  • • Discovered by Watson & Crick
Base Pairs
  • • Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T)
  • • Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C)

Chromosome Structure:

Chromatin

Loose form of DNA and proteins during interphase

Chromosome

Condensed chromatin visible during cell division

Sister Chromatids

Two identical copies joined at the centromere

🔢 Human Chromosomes:

Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs) in most cells. Gametes have 23 chromosomes (haploid).

Unit 3, Week 1, Day 1: DNA Structure

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Describe the components of DNA nucleotides
  • • Explain the double helix structure of DNA
  • • Understand Chargaff's rules and base pairing
  • • Compare DNA structure in prokaryotes and eukaryotes

🧬 The Blueprint of Life

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that stores genetic information in all living organisms. Its unique structure allows it to store vast amounts of information and pass it accurately from generation to generation.

DNA Nucleotides:

Each nucleotide consists of three parts:

🍬
Sugar (Deoxyribose)

5-carbon sugar backbone

⚡
Phosphate Group

Creates the backbone charge

🎯
Nitrogenous Base

A, T, G, or C

The Four Bases:

Purines (Double Ring)
  • • Adenine (A) - pairs with Thymine
  • • Guanine (G) - pairs with Cytosine
Pyrimidines (Single Ring)
  • • Thymine (T) - pairs with Adenine
  • • Cytosine (C) - pairs with Guanine

Double Helix Structure:

  • • Two antiparallel strands: Run in opposite directions (3' to 5')
  • • Complementary base pairing: A-T and G-C held by hydrogen bonds
  • • Major and minor grooves: Allow protein binding
  • • Right-handed helix: Discovered by Watson, Crick, and Franklin

Unit 3, Week 1, Day 2: DNA Replication

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Explain the semiconservative nature of DNA replication
  • • Identify key enzymes involved in DNA replication
  • • Describe the steps of DNA replication process
  • • Understand the directionality of DNA synthesis

🔄 Copying the Blueprint

DNA replication is the process by which DNA makes an exact copy of itself before cell division. This ensures that each new cell receives a complete set of genetic instructions.

Semiconservative Replication:

Each new DNA molecule contains:

  • • One original (conserved) strand
  • • One newly synthesized strand
  • • Proven by Meselson and Stahl experiment

Key Enzymes:

✂️ Helicase

Unwinds the double helix, breaking hydrogen bonds

🔗 DNA Polymerase

Adds new nucleotides in 5' to 3' direction

🧵 Primase

Creates RNA primers to start synthesis

🔧 Ligase

Joins Okazaki fragments on lagging strand

Replication Process:

  1. Initiation: Helicase unwinds DNA at origin of replication
  2. Elongation: DNA polymerase adds nucleotides to both strands
  3. Leading strand: Synthesized continuously
  4. Lagging strand: Synthesized in fragments (Okazaki fragments)
  5. Termination: Replication ends when entire molecule is copied

Unit 3, Week 1, Day 3: RNA Structure

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Compare and contrast RNA and DNA structure
  • • Identify the components of RNA nucleotides
  • • Explain why RNA is single-stranded
  • • Understand the significance of uracil in RNA

📋 The Messenger Molecule

RNA (Ribonucleic acid) is similar to DNA but has key differences that allow it to perform diverse functions in the cell, from carrying genetic information to catalyzing reactions.

RNA vs DNA Comparison:

🧬 DNA
  • • Double-stranded
  • • Deoxyribose sugar
  • • Bases: A, T, G, C
  • • Long-term storage
  • • Found in nucleus
📋 RNA
  • • Single-stranded
  • • Ribose sugar
  • • Bases: A, U, G, C
  • • Temporary functions
  • • Found throughout cell

RNA Nucleotide Components:

🍯
Ribose Sugar

Has OH group on 2' carbon (vs DNA's H)

⚡
Phosphate Group

Same as DNA - creates backbone

🎯
Nitrogenous Base

A, U, G, or C (U replaces T)

Why Single-Stranded?

  • • Flexibility: Can fold into complex 3D shapes
  • • Temporary function: Degrades quickly after use
  • • Base pairing: Can form hairpin loops and secondary structures
  • • Catalytic activity: Some RNAs act as enzymes (ribozymes)

Unit 3, Week 1, Day 4: Types of RNA

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • • Distinguish between mRNA, tRNA, and rRNA
  • • Explain the specific function of each RNA type
  • • Describe how different RNAs work together in protein synthesis
  • • Understand the concept of the central dogma

🎭 RNA's Many Roles

Different types of RNA have specialized functions in the cell. The three main types work together to convert genetic information from DNA into functional proteins.

📨 mRNA (Messenger RNA)
  • • Carries genetic code from DNA
  • • Template for protein synthesis
  • • Contains codons (3-base sequences)
  • • Processed in eukaryotes (capping, tailing)
  • • Short-lived in the cell
🚚 tRNA (Transfer RNA)
  • • Brings amino acids to ribosome
  • • Cloverleaf secondary structure
  • • Contains anticodon region
  • • Each tRNA specific for one amino acid
  • • About 70-90 nucleotides long
🏭 rRNA (Ribosomal RNA)
  • • Major component of ribosomes
  • • Catalyzes peptide bond formation
  • • Most abundant RNA in cell
  • • Highly conserved across species
  • • Forms ribosome structure

The Central Dogma:

DNA → RNA → Protein

Transcription

DNA → mRNA

Translation

mRNA → Protein

Working Together:

  1. mRNA carries the genetic message from DNA
  2. rRNA forms the ribosome where translation occurs
  3. tRNA brings the correct amino acids to the ribosome
  4. Together they create proteins based on genetic instructions
